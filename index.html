Planets

Earth

Planets are large celestial bodies orbiting a star, and have enough gravity to make them almost perfectly round and have cleared their orbital path of other debris. The Greek philosopher Ptolemy first coined the term "planet" in the 2nd century AD, meaning "wanderer", as they appeared to move across the sky differently from stars.

Our home planet, Earth, is third from the Sun, and rests on a razor's edge of habitability that makes life possible

Dwarf Planets

Pluto

Dwarf planets are celestial bodies that orbit the Sun and have enough mass to assume a nearly round shape, but have not cleared their orbital path of other debris. The term "dwarf planet" was coined by astronomer Alan Stern in 1991 and was officially adopted by the International Astronomical Union in 2006.

Pluto is a dwarf planet in the Kuiper belt, a ring of icy bodies beyond Neptune. It was discovered in 1930 by Clyde Tombaugh and was classified as the ninth planet until 2006 when the International Astronomical Union reclassified it as a dwarf planet.

Moons

The Moon

Moons, also known as natural satellites, are celestial bodies that orbit planets or dwarf planets. The term "moon" is derived from the Old English word "mōna", which means "to measure", and gave us words such as "month". Moons can vary greatly in size, composition, and geological activity.

The Moon is Earth's only natural satellite and the fifth largest moon in the solar system. It is about one-quarter the diameter of Earth and is located approximately 384,400 kilometers away. The Moon's gravitational influence is responsible for tides on Earth.

Stars

The Sun

Stars are massive, luminous spheres of plasma (hot gas, mostly hydrogen and helium) held together by gravity. The English word "star" comes from the Indo-European root "h₂stḗr-," which means "to burn" or "to shine."

The nearest star to Earth is the Sun, which is the source of most of the energy in our solar system. It is a nearly perfect sphere of hot plasma, with internal convective motion that generates a magnetic field via a dynamo process.

Galaxies

The Milky Way

Galaxies are vast collections of stars, gas, dust, and dark matter that are held together by gravity. The term "galaxy" comes from the Greek word "galaxias," which means "milky circle," referring to the appearance of our own galaxy, the Milky Way, as a milky band of light in the night sky.

The Milky Way is a barred spiral galaxy that contains our solar system. It is estimated to be about 100,000 light-years in diameter and contains between 100 and 400 billion stars. The Milky Way is part of a group of galaxies known as the Local Group, which also includes the Andromeda Galaxy and the Triangulum Galaxy.

Black Holes

Sagittarius A*

Black holes are regions of spacetime where gravity is so strong that nothing, not even light, can escape from them. The term "black hole" was coined by physicist John Archibald Wheeler in 1967, replacing older terms such as "frozen star" and "dark star". Black holes are formed when massive stars collapse under their own gravity at the end of their life cycles.

Sagittarius A* (pronounced "Sagittarius A-star") is a supermassive black hole located at the center of our Milky Way galaxy. It has a mass of about 4 million times that of our Sun and is located approximately 26,000 light-years from Earth. The name "Sagittarius A*" comes from its location in the constellation Sagittarius and the asterisk (*) indicates that it is a compact radio source.
